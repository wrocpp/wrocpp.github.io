On September 23, 1999, NASA lost a $327 million spacecraft to a unit conversion. The Mars Climate Orbiter's ground software, written by Lockheed Martin, reported thruster impulse in **pound-force seconds**. The trajectory software at JPL read those numbers as **newton seconds**. Every small correction burn was silently wrong by a factor of 4.45, for months, until the orbiter hit the Martian atmosphere 170 km too low and broke apart.

No exception was thrown. No assertion fired. The numbers were just numbers.

## Making the bug a type error

That is precisely the class of bug **mp-units** exists to kill. It is a modern C++ library (C++20 and later) in which a value's unit is part of its *type*, so a pound-force second and a newton second can never silently mix: they either convert **exactly and explicitly**, or the code does not compile.

Two things to notice in that demo. The conversion `small_forces.in(N * s)` produces `444.822 N s` from `100 s lbf` — the exact factor-4.45 relationship that killed the mission, handled by the type system instead of by a human remembering to multiply. And the commented-out last line — assigning a bare `100.0` to a newton-second quantity — is a **compile error**. There is no path from "raw number" to "quantity" without saying what the number means.

None of this costs anything at runtime. The dimensional analysis happens entirely at compile time; what remains in the binary is the same arithmetic you would have written by hand, minus the opportunity to get it wrong.

## Why a series, and why now

mp-units is worth more than one post, for three reasons.

First, it is deeper than "attach a unit to a double." It models the **International System of Quantities** (ISO 80000) faithfully enough to catch bugs that dimensional analysis alone cannot see — we will meet those in the next episode.

Second, it is on the standard track. The library is the reference implementation behind **P3045, "Quantities and units library,"** a candidate for **C++29**. Reading mp-units today is a preview of what may land in `std` itself.

Third, and closest to home for this publication: its lead author is **Mateusz Pusz**, a C++ architect from Gdańsk, an ISO C++ committee voting member since 2017, and a co-founder of the Polish subcommittee that made Poland a voting member of the committee. The strongest bid to put physical units into the C++ standard library is being led from Poland.

Over the coming weeks we will walk the library's big ideas, one runnable demo at a time: quantity kinds, the affine space, one-line custom units, unit-safe formatting, and finally the road to C++29.
