`std::flat_map` has been making the rounds again, usually pitched as "a faster `std::map`." The pitch is not wrong, but it hides the design, and the design is what tells you when the container fits.

C++23's `std::flat_map` (P0429, together with `flat_set`, `flat_multimap`, and `flat_multiset`) is not a node-based structure at all. It is a **container adaptor over two sequences**: a sorted vector of keys and a parallel vector of values, kept in lockstep. Lookup is binary search over contiguous keys. There is no tree, no per-node allocation, no parent pointers — and the API does not pretend otherwise: `keys()` and `values()` hand you the underlying containers, and construction can *adopt* pre-sorted data without doing any work.

The demo shows the whole mental model in three prints. The `keys()` observer returns `["dns", "http", "https", "ssh"]` — sorted, contiguous, exactly what binary search wants. `values()` is the parallel array. And the last construction is the part most introductions skip: `std::sorted_unique` plus two moved-in vectors builds the map by **taking ownership of your containers as-is**. If your data arrives sorted — from a file, a database, a build step — you pay nothing at all. (The example also runs on clang with libc++; both mainstream standard libraries ship it.)

## What the two vectors buy you

- **Cache-friendly lookups.** A `std::map` lookup chases pointers through nodes scattered across the heap; a `flat_map` lookup walks a contiguous array the prefetcher understands. For lookup-heavy workloads this is routinely a large win.
- **Memory.** No per-node allocation, no left/right/parent pointers, no allocator bookkeeping per element. Two vectors' worth of data, full stop.
- **Free bulk construction** via `sorted_unique`, and symmetric `extract()` to take the vectors back out.

## What they cost you

This is the part the enthusiasm posts tend to skip, and it is a real contract change, not fine print:

- **Insertion into a populated map is O(n)** — everything after the insertion point shifts, in both vectors. Build once, look up often, or batch your mutations.
- **Iterators AND references are invalidated** by insert and erase. A `std::map` promises stability; `flat_map` promises the opposite. Code that holds references into the map does not port.
- **Exception behavior is unusual**: if a mutating operation throws, the standard guarantees the invariants (sorted keys, equal lengths) are restored — in some cases *by clearing the container*. Mutating a `flat_map` under exceptions is not the transaction you may be used to.

And one framing correction: `flat_map`'s competitor is `std::map`, not `std::unordered_map`. A good hash table still wins pure point lookups on large data; what `flat_map` adds is ordered iteration, no hashing requirements, and the memory story — at tree-map prices it beats handily.

If your data is mostly static and read a lot — configuration, symbol tables, routing tables, the classic cases — the two-vector design is exactly the trick you would have hand-rolled anyway. Now it is in the box, with the sharp edges documented.
