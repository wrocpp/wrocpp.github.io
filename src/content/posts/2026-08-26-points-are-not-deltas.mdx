Here is a bug no unit checker from the last episode can catch: add two temperature *readings*. 21 °C + 17 °C. Same unit, same dimension, same quantity kind — and still nonsense. "The sum of this morning's temperature and this afternoon's" is not a physical quantity; nothing in the world is 38 °C because of it.

The mathematics has had a name for this distinction for a century: an **affine space**. Some values are *points* — positions against an origin: a temperature reading, a timestamp, an altitude. Some values are *deltas* — differences between points: a temperature change, a duration, a climb. Deltas form a vector space, so you may add them freely. Points do not: you can subtract two points (getting a delta), or add a delta to a point (getting a new point), and that is the complete list.

`std::chrono` users already know this split as `time_point` vs `duration`. mp-units generalizes it to *every* quantity.

## quantity_point vs quantity

<GodboltEmbed id="hsjbssx8b" gccOnly title="Point + delta compiles; point + point does not" gcc_subtitle="x86-64 GCC 16.1 · -std=c++23 -O2 · mp-units trunk · runs live" />

The vocabulary maps one-to-one onto the physics: `point<deg_C>(17.0)` is a reading, `delta<deg_C>(4.5)` is a change. Adding the change to the reading yields a new reading; subtracting two readings yields a change; and `afternoon.quantity_from(si::ice_point)` asks the only question an absolute reading can honestly answer — *how far from a stated origin?* The two commented lines, adding two readings and assigning a point to a delta, are compile errors.

Temperatures make the sharpest example because their everyday unit hides an origin (0 °C *is* a point — the ice point), but the same modeling covers timestamps vs durations, positions vs displacements, and altitudes vs climbs. Anywhere your code passes "a metre" around, it is worth asking: a metre *from where*, or a metre *of difference*? mp-units lets the signature say which, and the compiler holds the line.
